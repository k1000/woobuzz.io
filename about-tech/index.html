Building Wobuzz Radio: A Technical Deep Dive into AI-Powered Slow Radio

The Problem Space

Most music platforms today optimize for endless scrolling and short-term engagement. Algorithms feed you what's popular rather than what's interesting. But what if we could use AI not to accelerate consumption, but to slow it down—to recreate the experience of classic radio where knowledgeable presenters guided you through musical landscapes with expertise and passion?

That's the challenge I set out to solve with Wobuzz Radio: a Python-based system that creates themed radio shows from local music collections, complete with AI-generated presenter introductions, artist research, and cover art—all optimized for deep listening rather than algorithmic churn.

Architecture Overview

Wobuzz Radio is built as a modular CLI application with a clear separation of concerns:

Scanner → Producer → Mixer → Publisher

The system exposes 35 MCP (Model Context Protocol) tools, making it compatible with Claude Desktop and other AI assistants. This architectural decision transformed Wobuzz Radio from a standalone CLI into an AI-native application that can be orchestrated through natural language.

Three AI Subagents: Division of Labor

Wobuzz Radio's intelligence is distributed across three specialized AI subagents, each with distinct responsibilities:

1. Show Producer

The orchestrator that researches themes, selects tracks, and designs the overall show arc. It conducts parallel web research (6 concurrent searches), matches themes to series personalities, and curates track sequences that flow musically and narratively. Think of it as the executive producer who shapes the show's identity.

2. Show Presenter

The voice and personality of the show. Each of the 15 presenter profiles has unique expertise, speaking style, and tonal characteristics. The presenter generates all commentary, introductions, and transitions in their distinctive voice—whether that's Miles' "cool-headed innovator" persona for fusion shows or Klaus' "cosmic traveler" voice for krautrock journeys.

3. Cover Art Designer

The visual interpreter that generates minimalist, genre-appropriate cover art. Using AI image generation (x.ai Grok-2 or Black Forest Labs FLUX), it translates musical themes into visual aesthetics—black-and-white compositions for jazz, cosmic imagery for psychedelia, refined typography for classical.

This separation of concerns enables parallel execution (producer researches while designer generates visuals) and specialization (each agent optimized for its domain).

The Presenter-as-First-Class-Entity Pattern

One of the most interesting technical decisions was treating AI presenters as first-class entities with persistent identity, personality, and expertise.

Traditional approach: Generate TTS on-demand with ad-hoc voice settings Wobuzz approach: Define presenters as reusable profiles with identity, voice, personality, and expertise

Each presenter is defined in JSON with four key domains:

json { "id": "miles", "name": "Miles", "voice": { "tts_provider": "cartesia", "voice_id": "79f8b5fb-2cc8-479a-80df-29f7a7cf1a3e" }, "personality": { "description": "A cool-headed innovator who bridges musical worlds", "traits": ["knowledgeable", "innovative", "articulate"], "speaking_style": "Speaks with measured confidence" }, "expertise": { "genres": ["fusion", "jazz", "experimental"], "era": "1960s-1990s", "regions": ["North America", "Global"] } }

This separation enables powerful composition: 18 series configurations × 15 presenter profiles = unlimited show variations. Series define what (theme, genre, visual identity), while presenters define who (voice, personality, expertise).

Multi-Provider AI Integration

Wobuzz Radio abstracts both TTS and image generation behind provider interfaces, enabling runtime selection and graceful fallback:

TTS Providers:

  • Cartesia (primary, 15 presenters configured)
  • ElevenLabs (high-quality alternative)
  • Google TTS (free fallback)
  • Voxtral (experimental)

Image Providers:

  • Nanobanana - Gemini model
  • x.ai Grok-2 (Aurora model)
  • Black Forest Labs (FLUX.1.1-pro)

The Show Creation Workflow

The show creation process consists of six distinct phases:

  • Genre history and cultural context
  • Musical characteristics
  • Key artists and pioneers
  • Artist interviews and quotes
  • Album creation stories
  • Critical reception

This research informs both series selection and commentary quality.

Real-World Results

  • 60-minute shows with 6-10 curated tracks + AI commentary
  • Mixcloud-compliant content structure (max 3 songs per album, 4 per artist)
  • Fully automated publishing to static website with RSS feed
  • Sub-60-second creation time for parallel-optimized workflows

Architecture Lessons Learned

  1. Treat AI personas as persistent entities: Reusable presenter profiles are more maintainable than ad-hoc TTS calls.

  2. Optimize for parallel execution: Identify independent operations and batch them into single execution rounds.

  3. Provider abstraction is essential: AI APIs change pricing, fail, and improve—decouple from specific providers.

  4. MCP as integration layer: Standardized protocols transform standalone tools into AI-composable services.

  5. Separation of semantic and technical: Series define what to create, presenters define how to speak—mixing these leads to coupling.

What's Next

Wobuzz Radio demonstrates that AI can enhance cultural experiences without replacing human curation. The architecture proves that:

  • AI-native applications benefit from protocol-based integration (MCP)
  • Performance optimization requires identifying parallelizable operations
  • Reusable personas are more powerful than disposable generations
  • Provider abstraction enables graceful degradation and cost optimization

The project is open-source and actively developed. If you're exploring AI integration patterns beyond chatbots, or building audio/music applications with AI, I'd love to hear your thoughts.
